On-page SEO is where you have the most direct control over your rankings. Unlike backlinks, which depend on other sites linking to you, or technical factors, which often require developer involvement, on-page elements are things you can optimize right now. A systematic on-page audit identifies exactly which elements need updating across your site.

This guide covers the six core areas of an on-page SEO audit: title tags, meta descriptions, heading structure, content quality, keyword targeting, and internal linking.

## Title Tags

Title tags are the most important on-page ranking factor. They appear as the clickable headline in search results and tell both search engines and users what a page is about.

A title tag audit checks several things across every indexable page. Each title must be unique--duplicate titles confuse search engines and waste ranking potential. Each should include the primary keyword, ideally near the beginning, while remaining readable and compelling to humans. Length matters too: keep titles under roughly 60 characters to avoid truncation in SERPs, though pixel width is the real constraint.

Look for pages with missing titles entirely, titles that are auto-generated from templates without human review, and titles that read like keyword stuffing rather than natural language. Pages targeting similar keywords should have clearly differentiated titles to prevent cannibalization.

## Meta Descriptions

Meta descriptions do not directly influence rankings, but they heavily influence click-through rates. A well-written meta description acts as free ad copy between your title tag and the rest of the search results page.

Audit meta descriptions for uniqueness across pages. Generic, duplicated, or auto-generated descriptions waste the opportunity to sell your page to searchers. Each description should summarize the page's value proposition in roughly 150 to 160 characters, include the primary keyword naturally, and include a subtle call to action where appropriate.

Also check for pages with no meta description at all. Google will auto-generate a snippet from the page content in these cases, but the result is often awkwardly truncated or missing the most persuasive text.

## Heading Structure

Headings organize content for readers and signal topic hierarchy to search engines. Every page should have exactly one H1 that states the main topic. Subheadings should cascade logically from H2s to H3s without skipping levels.

Common heading issues include multiple H1 tags on a single page, H2s that are vague or purely stylistic rather than descriptive, and headings that miss the opportunity to include secondary keywords. An audit should verify that each H2 addresses a distinct subtopic and that the overall heading structure mirrors a logical outline of the page.

## Content Quality

Content quality goes beyond word count. An on-page audit evaluates whether each page actually satisfies the search intent behind its target keyword.

Check for thin content--pages that offer little substantive information beyond a few paragraphs. Look for outdated information that damages credibility, factual claims presented without supporting evidence, and content that regurgitates the top-ranking pages without adding original insight.

Readability is also a content quality factor. Very long paragraphs, jargon-heavy language, and poor formatting all drive users away. An audit should flag pages where the reading experience could be improved through better structure, shorter paragraphs, or clearer language.

## Keyword Targeting

Each page should target a primary keyword and a handful of closely related secondary keywords. An audit checks whether those keywords appear in the right places: the title tag, the first 100 words of body text, at least one H2, the URL slug, and naturally throughout the content.

Equally important is checking for keyword cannibalization--multiple pages targeting the same primary keyword. When two or more pages compete for the same term, search engines split authority between them and neither ranks as well as one consolidated page could.

Review Search Console data to see which queries each page currently ranks for. If a page ranks for many irrelevant or low-value queries, it may need better keyword focus or a content rewrite.

## Internal Linking

Internal links distribute page authority through your site and help users discover related content. An on-page audit should check that each important page receives links from relevant, contextually appropriate anchor text.

Look for orphan pages that have no internal links at all, making them nearly invisible to crawlers. Identify pages that could benefit from additional internal links from high-authority pages on your site. Check that anchor text varies naturally and describes the linked page accurately rather than using generic phrases like "click here."

Also review your navigation and footer links to ensure they surface the most valuable pages without creating link bloat. Every internal link should serve either a user need or a crawl efficiency purpose.

Run this on-page audit across your entire site and prioritize fixes by impact: title tags and content quality first, then heading structure and keyword targeting, followed by meta descriptions and internal linking refinements.
