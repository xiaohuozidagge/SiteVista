Expanding a website to serve multiple countries or languages introduces a layer of complexity that domestic SEO does not have to contend with. International SEO is not just about translation--it is about ensuring that the right version of each page appears to the right users in the right markets, and that search engines understand the relationship between your regional and language variants.

An international SEO audit systematically checks every element of your global website configuration. Here is what to examine across five critical areas.

## Hreflang Implementation

Hreflang tags are the primary mechanism for telling search engines which language and regional audience each page is intended for. Despite their importance, hreflang implementations are among the most commonly broken elements in international SEO.

A thorough hreflang audit checks for several failure modes. Confirm that every page that has hreflang annotations receives reciprocal annotations from every page it references. If the English page points to the French page, the French page must point back to the English page. Broken reciprocity is one of the most frequent causes of hreflang-related ranking confusion.

Verify that hreflang codes use the correct format: language code alone for language targeting, or language plus country code for regional targeting. Check that all referenced URLs are canonical, return a 200 status, and are not blocked by robots.txt. Hreflang tags pointing to redirected or noindexed pages will be ignored.

Also audit for the self-referential hreflang tag. Each page should include an hreflang annotation for its own language and region combination. Missing self-references are a common oversight that can cause hreflang clusters to break.

## Geo-Targeting

Geo-targeting helps search engines understand which country a page or site section is intended to serve. Google uses several signals for this, and your audit should verify they are all aligned.

If you are using country-code top-level domains like .co.uk or .de, those inherently signal geographic targeting. For sites on generic top-level domains like .com, Google Search Console's International Targeting report lets you set a country preference. Audit this setting to confirm it matches your actual market targeting.

Server location is a minor geo-signal. While it carries less weight than it once did, hosting your site in the country you are targeting can provide a modest relevance boost and often improves page speed for local users.

Local signals like local business schema, NAP consistency on location pages, and local backlinks also reinforce geo-targeting. An international audit should check these signals across each market you serve.

## Multi-Language Structure

How you organize multi-language content affects both user experience and crawl efficiency. The three common URL structures each have tradeoffs.

Country-code top-level domains provide the strongest geo-signal but are the most expensive and operationally complex to maintain. Subdomains like fr.example.com are easier to set up but do not inherit the full authority of the main domain. Subdirectories like example.com/fr/ consolidate authority under one domain and are generally the easiest to maintain.

Your audit should verify that the chosen structure is implemented consistently. Mixed structures--using subdomains for some languages and subdirectories for others--create confusion for both users and search engines.

Check that language and regional variants are properly linked through navigation elements that users can find and use. Avoid automatic redirects based on IP address or browser language without giving users the option to override and stay on their chosen version. Forced redirects can prevent search engines from crawling alternate language versions.

## International Content

Translation quality directly affects ranking and user experience. Machine-translated content without human review reads poorly, and poorly translated pages have high bounce rates that signal low quality to search engines.

An international content audit checks that translated content is properly localized, not just translated. Localization means adapting cultural references, currency and measurement units, date formats, and examples to be relevant to the target audience. A case study about a US-based scenario may not resonate with users in Japan or Germany.

Verify that key on-page elements are translated and optimized for each market. Title tags, meta descriptions, and H1 headings should be translated and keyword-researched independently per language--not just direct translations of the primary language versions. A keyword that performs well in English may have a completely different search volume and intent pattern in another language.

## Common International SEO Issues

Several problems surface repeatedly in international SEO audits. Being aware of them helps you spot them faster.

**Hreflang pointing to redirected URLs** wastes the annotation. The hreflang must point to the final canonical URL. If your French page redirects from an old URL, update the hreflang to reference the destination, not the redirect.

**Missing language annotations for key pages.** Many sites implement hreflang on their blog or core pages but forget about resource pages, tools, or documentation that drive significant international traffic.

**Canonical tags that conflict with hreflang.** A page that is canonicalized to a different language version while also being included in hreflang clusters sends contradictory signals. Canonical and hreflang should agree.

**Inconsistent navigation between language versions.** If the English site has a resource section that the Spanish version omits, Spanish-speaking users lose access to content that could be valuable and converted.

An international SEO audit is more detail-intensive than a standard audit, but the payoff is proportional: markets you enter correctly compound your traffic from day one, while incorrectly configured international setups can fragment your authority across duplicate content for years.
